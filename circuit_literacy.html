Introduction
How to use this guide

This guide teaches you to read DC control circuits the way a protection engineer reads them — not by memorizing drawings, but by understanding why every device is there. When you know the reason, the circuit becomes legible on any project, not just the ones you've worked on.

Talk-first method
Each section is structured for the talk-first workflow. Read the overview, then close the guide and explain the circuit out loud — to Claude, to a colleague, or to yourself. The act of explaining exposes the gaps. Come back to the guide only for what you couldn't explain.

The three circuits in this guide were chosen because they chain together. Circuit 1 is the foundation. Circuit 2 builds on it by adding an auxiliary relay. Circuit 3 uses Circuit 1 as its initiating event. Master Circuit 1 and the other two come faster than you expect.

How to read a DC elementary drawing

A DC elementary (also called a DC schematic or control elementary) shows how control current flows to operate a circuit breaker. Before looking at specific circuits, understand the page layout.

The universal rule
Positive DC bus runs along the top of the drawing. Negative DC bus runs along the bottom. Every device in the circuit hangs between them. Current flows from top to bottom — positive bus → devices → negative bus — whenever a complete path exists.

This guide redraws those circuits left-to-right instead of top-to-bottom, because the left-to-right flow maps directly to the sequence of events during a fault. Once you understand the sequence, translating to the ladder-style drawing on a real DC elementary is straightforward.

Devices in these diagrams are drawn as simplified schematic symbols. Hover or tap any device in the interactive diagrams to see its IEEE device number, its full name, and what it does in the circuit.

Contact types explained

Understanding contact types is half the battle in reading any DC elementary. Every contact in these circuits is one of the following:

Normally Open (NO)
Open when the operating device is de-energized or in its resting state. Closes when the device picks up or operates. Most relay output contacts are normally open — they close to pass current when the relay trips.
Normally Closed (NC)
Closed when the operating device is in its resting state. Opens when the device operates. The 52b contact is normally closed — the breaker is normally closed, so the 52b contact is normally open. Wait — let that settle. The breaker being "closed" means current is flowing through the primary circuit. The 52b auxiliary contact is "normally closed" meaning it's closed when the breaker itself is closed. When the breaker opens (trips), the 52b contact opens, interrupting the trip coil current.
The 52a / 52b distinction
52a = closed when the breaker is CLOSED (a for "armed" — the breaker is ready to carry current). 52b = closed when the breaker is OPEN (b for "broken" — the breaker has broken the circuit). They are always in opposite states. On a drawing, you'll see these labeled with the breaker designation: for breaker CB-1, they're labeled 52a-CB1 and 52b-CB1.
Part 2
Circuit 1: Simple Overcurrent Trip

This is the foundational circuit. Every other trip circuit in this guide is Circuit 1 with something added. Master this one completely — the rest follows.

The circuit connects a single overcurrent relay output contact to the trip coil of a circuit breaker. When the relay detects an overcurrent condition and its output contact closes, DC current flows from the positive bus through the contact, through the trip coil, and back to the negative bus. The trip coil energizes, the breaker opens, and the 52b contact immediately opens the trip coil circuit to prevent burnout.

Device by device — what happens during a fault
  1. 1Fault occurs on the system. AC current rises above the relay pickup setpoint. The 51 element starts its inverse-time timer — the higher the fault current, the faster it counts.
  2. 251 relay output contact closes. After the time delay, the relay's normally-open output contact closes. DC current can now flow from the positive bus, through the fuse, through the 51 contact.
  3. 3Current flows through the 52b contact. The breaker is currently closed, so the 52b contact is also closed. DC current continues through to the trip coil.
  4. 4Trip coil energizes. The electromagnet pulls its plunger, releasing the breaker's stored mechanical energy. The breaker contacts begin to open.
  5. 552b contact opens. As soon as the breaker mechanism moves, the 52b auxiliary contact opens — cutting DC current to the trip coil. The coil is protected from sustained current that would burn it out.
  6. 6Breaker is now open. The fault current is interrupted. The relay resets when it no longer sees overcurrent. The trip coil is de-energized. The circuit is ready for the next operation.
The 52b question: what happens without it?
Think through this before reading the answer
If the 52b contact were removed and the trip coil were wired directly from the 51 contact to the negative bus — what happens after the breaker opens?

Without the 52b contact: the relay output contact closes → the trip coil energizes → the breaker opens. So far, so good. But now the relay sees no more fault current — it resets, and its output contact opens. The trip coil de-energizes. Problem solved? Not quite.

The relay contact is small and not rated to interrupt DC current through an inductive load (the trip coil is an inductor). When the relay contact tries to open with current flowing, it arcs. On a small relay contact, this arc erodes the contact surface. Repeated operations destroy the relay output contact.

Worse: some relays hold their output contacts closed longer than the breaker takes to open. In that case, the trip coil stays energized for the full duration — hundreds of milliseconds — drawing 2–5 amps continuously. Trip coils are rated for momentary duty only. Sustained current at that level burns the coil out.

Why 52b solves both problems
The 52b contact is a heavy-duty auxiliary contact rated to interrupt DC current. It opens as soon as the breaker mechanism moves — faster than the relay output contact could open, and rated for the job. The relay contact opens with zero current flowing (the 52b has already interrupted it). Both the relay contact and the trip coil are protected.

Circuit 2: Transformer Differential with Auxiliary Relay

Circuit 2 is Circuit 1 with an important addition: an auxiliary relay (the 86 lockout) sits between the 87T differential relay output and the trip coil. This addition solves a problem that Circuit 1 doesn't have to deal with — the need to trip multiple breakers simultaneously, provide a visible fault indicator, and prevent automatic re-energization.

Why an auxiliary relay is needed
The 87T relay's output contact is a small, low-current rated contact. The trip coil draws 2–5 amps. Wiring the 87T contact directly to the trip coil would work once — then the contact surface would erode. The auxiliary relay (86 or 94) has a heavy-duty contact rated for DC trip coil current. The small 87T contact operates the auxiliary relay coil; the auxiliary relay's heavy contacts do the actual tripping.

In the diagram below, trace two paths from the 86 relay contacts — one to each breaker. This is how a single 87T relay operation simultaneously trips the high-side breaker and the low-side breaker.

86 lockout vs. 94 trip auxiliary — when each is used
86 Lockout Relay (manual reset)
Used for serious faults where you want to prevent automatic re-energization and force investigation. Transformer internal faults (87T), generator protection, bus faults. If the 86 has operated, someone must physically walk to the panel, investigate, and reset the relay before the breaker can be reclosed. This is a deliberate barrier against reclosing into a damaged transformer.
94 Trip Auxiliary Relay (self-resetting)
Used when automatic reset is acceptable — for example, a relay that trips for a transient condition that may have cleared. After the fault clears and the initiating relay resets, the 94 relay also resets automatically. It provides the same heavy-duty contact function as the 86 without the manual reset requirement. Common for overcurrent backup protection where reclosing is allowed.
Why both breakers must trip

A transformer has two (or more) terminals — a high-voltage winding and a low-voltage winding. Current can flow into the transformer from either direction. An internal fault can be fed from both sides simultaneously.

If only one breaker trips
Imagine the fault is fed from both the HV system and the LV bus. If only the HV breaker trips, the fault is still being fed from the LV side. The transformer continues to be destroyed. The protection has failed at its fundamental job — isolating the faulted equipment.

The 86 relay's multiple output contacts make it mechanically simple to trip both breakers from a single relay operation. No logic, no communication — just multiple contacts wired to multiple trip coils, all closing at the same instant.

Part 4
Circuit 3: Breaker Failure Initiation

Breaker failure (BF) protection answers a specific question: what if the circuit breaker receives a trip signal and fails to open? Without BF protection, a faulted line or transformer that can't be cleared by its local breaker would continue to be fed until upstream protection sees the fault — which may take several seconds and cause far more damage and system disturbance.

The fundamental principle
Two conditions must both be true for breaker failure to complete: (1) enough time has elapsed for the breaker to have tripped, and (2) current is still flowing through the breaker — meaning it has not opened. This is an AND gate, implemented with a timer relay (62BF) and a current detector (50BF).

The AND logic — why both conditions matter
Why not just use the timer alone?
If BF completed on timer expiration alone, it would operate every time a protection relay issues a trip — even when the breaker operates correctly. The BF would trip the entire bus zone on every normal fault. The 50BF current supervision prevents this: if the breaker opened, current stopped, the 50BF contact opened, and the BF scheme resets regardless of the timer.
Why not just use the current detector alone?
Fault current flows continuously from the moment of fault until the breaker opens. If BF operated the instant current was detected, it would trip the bus zone before the local breaker even had a chance to operate. The 62BF timer provides the waiting period — a window of time for the breaker to do its job. Only after that window expires, if current is still flowing, do we conclude the breaker has failed.
The result
The timer and the current detector together implement an AND gate: time has elapsed AND current is still present. Both conditions must be true simultaneously. This is logic implemented with two contacts in series — no microprocessor required, though modern relays implement the same logic digitally.

Why does BF trip more breakers than the original fault trip? Because the local breaker has failed — it cannot interrupt current. The only way to clear the fault is to isolate the entire zone that feeds the failed breaker. That means tripping every other breaker connected to the same bus section. This is a major system disturbance by design — BF is the last line of defense before the fault is fed indefinitely.

The fault chain — how the three circuits connect

These three circuits are not independent. A single fault event can trigger all three in sequence. Understanding the chain is the final step in circuit literacy.

Normal fault — Circuits 1 and 2 only
A fault occurs on a transformer. The 87T relay detects it → operates the 86 lockout relay (Circuit 2) → 86 contacts trip both the HV and LV breakers. Simultaneously, the BF initiation contact starts the 62BF timer (Circuit 3). The HV breaker opens successfully → fault current drops → 50BF contact opens → BF scheme resets without completing. Total time: 3–5 cycles (50–80 ms) to breaker opening.
Failed breaker — all three circuits in sequence
Same fault. Same 87T operation. The HV breaker fails to open — mechanical failure, trip coil failure, or the breaker is already in an indeterminate state. Fault current continues to flow. The 50BF element sees current. When the 62BF timer expires (150 ms), both contacts in the BF trip rung are closed. The 86BF lockout relay operates. The bus zone breakers all trip. The transformer is finally isolated — 200+ ms after the fault began. The system has lost more generation or load than a normal fault would have caused.
Reading an unfamiliar DC elementary

You will encounter DC elementaries on projects where you didn't write the drawings. Every one of them uses the vocabulary and logic you've learned here. When you pick up an unfamiliar drawing:

  1. 1Find the positive and negative DC buses. These are always at the edges of the drawing — top/bottom in ladder style, or identified by bus labels. Every circuit hangs between them.
  2. 2Find the trip coils (TC). Work backwards from the trip coil to find what controls it. This always leads you to the trip circuit logic.
  3. 3Find the 52b contact in series with each trip coil. Confirm it's there — if it isn't, ask why.
  4. 4Identify whether there is an auxiliary relay (86 or 94) in the path. If yes, find the coil and all of its output contacts. Each output contact is a separate trip or seal-in action.
  5. 5Find the initiation contact — the protection relay output that starts the whole chain. This is always a normally-open contact. The IEEE device number tells you which relay and function it is.
  6. 6If you see a timer (62) and a current detector (50) in series, you're looking at a breaker failure trip rung. Work backwards to find the initiation circuit and forwards to find the zone trip targets.
The mental model
Every DC control circuit is a path from +DC to −DC. Devices in series are logic AND gates — all must be closed for current to flow. Devices in parallel are OR gates — any one closed is enough. Once you see the drawing as a series of AND and OR gates implementing protection logic, no drawing is unfamiliar — only the specific devices and their settings are new.
